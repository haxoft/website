Moreover we should keep in mind that the pitch is coming closer and closer and that means, we should start also to catch some bullet points for our solution description in our final report for the planspiel.

    As we already mentioned, the development of the project still continues. We managed to add user management to the backend and the frontend of the application, as well as the corresponding session management. This functionality allows the HXT to recognize different users (with different access rights) and to serve resources to these users accordingly. One part of this functionality that was especially time-consuming was the authentication of users (user agents) making requests to the HXT plugin. The user management is handled at the "front-line" by Atlassian and our plugin just simply receives a "claim" containing the information about the user making the request. This kind of delegated authentication was a bit confusing at the beginning since it comprises 3 entities (Atlassian, HXT, User agent) sending and receiving a JWT.

We have found the first two optimized IR queries for extraction of our first two requirements (which are both very big and ugly creatures) and we are currently trying to insert them in the extraction module of the HXT plugin.

    The team meeting this week delivered some promising results. As a result of our brainstorming, we were able to come with some very promising ideas for the core module of the HXT - the module responsible for the requirements extraction. After some research and discussions we have come up with two different approaches of how to deal with the core functionality - one uses Information Retrieval and the other Information Extraction. Despite their similar names, the two approaches are quite different. One is more related to Natural Language Processing, and the other is rather based on full-text indexing. In the following weeks, we should elaborate a "proof of concept" example for each of them and after that, we will be able to decide for the most promising in terms of time consumption vs effectiveness.
    As already mentioned, the second approach is based on Information Extraction(IE) and potentially more complex and time-consuming. In an ideal world, we would have more time (at least more than 3 months) for the implementation and we would be able to use both approaches in the HXT. The reality, however, is that we have to deliver a running MVP at the middle of March, which is quite restrictive in terms of time possibilities.

    Apart from researching about different approaches to solve our main problem, we also looked around for some python libraries which would help us with the task. Moreover, we collected and agreed upon some of the most relevant requirements that the HXT plugin should be able to extract. It is a long list and we are not sure that we can deliver them all before the final pitch day, but we will definitely try hard.
    We were aware of the fact that in case we decide ourselves for the IR approach, we will have to build several queries that would match each type of requirement. For this reason, we started to sketch such queries - mainly for the most common and simple requirements. It is worth mentioning that our list of the top-priority requirements to extract was previously consulted with one of our early "potential customer"/stakeholder/interviewee - a member of the VSR team. We arranged a brief consultation in order to find out whether our list was accurate, and did not contain unnecessary / irrelevant requirements.

    The second approach is Information Extraction(IE). This approach is more about extracting knowledge from text. It uses classifiers, named entity recognition, relationship extraction, and other fancy methods belonging to AI. Both methods could be very useful in our future efforts to solve the problem of requirements extraction.

    9th - 15th January '17

    This weeks customer meeting was quite extensive! The consultation with Prof. Gaedke lasted about an hour. Apparently, there were many things we had to discuss. We presented him the first version of our clickable prototype, the first version of our business plan and the results of our research regarding calls for proposals.
    We shared with him our idea of building a desktop application and showed him our clickable prototype. He did not like very much the idea of building a "thick" desktop client. We were told that students of a Web Engineering program should be building web applications, not desktop apps. We have been obviously discussing inside the team, the different types of applications we could build, but none of us saw enough arguments for building a web app. The professor, however, seemed to have many arguments about why we should build a web application instead. We must say most of them were quite convincing. Among some of the arguments he shared with us was that distributing a desktop app is more difficult than simply creating an account in a web app, or adding a plugin to an existing platform like "Wordpress" or "Atlassian". Another argument was the functionality of shared projects. It is much easier to share projects in a web app than a desktop client.

    One very good point the Prof. made, was his suggestion about creating an application as a part of an already existing, well-established ecosystem. A plugin or extension that could leverage the success of a third party product, by using its existing plugin distribution system such as the Atlassian marketplace, Microsoft Windows Store app, Google play or similar application distribution centers. This was a very good idea which we readily adopted.
    We left from this meeting convinced that it would really be a better choice to build a web application. We will have to slightly change the prototype design, but not entirely. The structure will remain the same, but some components will have to be replaced in order to achieve a better "web" feeling.

    Somewhere in the middle of the meeting, the Prof. asked us, if our business "scale". We did not know what he meant, nor what to answer. We understood the importance of the question after he explained what he exactly meant. Put simply, a business scales, if it can growth revenue with minimal or no increase in operating costs. This question was tightly bound to his suggestion about exploiting an existing ecosystem. We could increase our growth without increasing the costs required for marketing. Having learned something new and interesting, we continued with the interview.

    To summarize our progress over the week, we can state that we conducted some research and held a couple of brainstorming sessions about the possible platforms we could use to build our application. After that, we concluded that Atlassian is the best option. We chose this platform for its prevalence among research groups as well as its appealing plugin architecture. The architecture is surprisingly flexible. The plugin (add-on in the Atlassian terminology) is a common web application that serves HTML content over the internet to the Atlassian product. The served content is then inserted in an Atlassian "IFrame" and served to the user as if it was content from Atlassian. This provides a seamless integration of the add-on and the Atlassian product, and at the same time provides an extreme level of flexibility.
    Another advantage of building the add-on as a web application is that we could very easily extend the system in order to publish a standalone version of it. The UI might remain unchanged, as well as the backend. The only modification we would have to make would be regarding the authentication of users. We would have to provide our own user management since the add-on uses delegated auth provided by Atlassian. This, however, is not at all difficult nor costly.

    During the meeting, we also showed the Prof. a SWOT Analysis we created with the help of a tool available at cayenneapps.com. While doing the analysis, we gained an interesting insight into our emerging company. The analysis forced us to think about our current situation from an external point of view.

    After the meeting, we managed to develop the first version of the user interface. We also created the skeleton of our Django server and configured a Postgres database. We also managed to do two more interviews with the VSRians and started with our research about NLP. This topic seems to be more extensive than we initially thought.

    In order to start with the interviews, we need to prepare a set of questions. For this purpose, we assembled a brainstorming session and wrote down our first stakeholder questionnaire. At the beginning, we ask the interviewee a set of questions regarding his personal experience and his current position. We think that this information could help us to classify the interviews and to decide which information is more relevant if we found that we received two contradictory answers. As a secondary side effect, it might be a good beginning that could help to diminish the tension that is always present in such interviews. After the initial session, we ask the interviewee what are the processes he/she applies when planning a project and whether he/she applies some standardized approach like the LFA. If they follow no standardized plan, then we ask about the process they currently apply including the description of the single tasks like: what are the inputs/outputs of each phase, what tools are used, what are the requirements to determine that a phase has been finished, etc.
    The following interview section deals with the usage of tools. We want to ask what are the tools they use and what are the pain points they experience when using these tools. We will try to collect information to discover the phases that are poorly, or not supported at all by software tools.
    At the end of the interview, we ask our interviewee to grade each phase and tool with a number from 1 to 5. This should help us to uncover the most painful spots of project planners, even if not mentioned directly.

After many discussions and brainstorming sessions, we have finally found common ground and decided for one name. As you probably know now, we have decided to name our team and company - 'Haxoft'. But why Haxoft?
    Haxoft is a composition of two words - hack and soft. While the reason for choosing soft is probably obvious, the reason why we use 'hack' might not be so. The word „hack“ is used in many contexts nowadays, but originally it means „to produce an ingenious piece of work that solves a meaningful problem“. And that’s what we stand for: game-changing and reliable software that will give you an advantage over your competitors. Curiously enough, the name "Haxoft" was one of the first names that came to our mind. One might, therefore, say we are using a rather spontaneous company name. To give you an example of some of the names that we were considering, we provide you below a brief list with some of them:

    • Hexoft
